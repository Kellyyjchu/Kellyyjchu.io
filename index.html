Mythic Art & Mental Health

Nikita Gill’s sixth volume of poetry “changes” the “lens” through which readers experience ancient myths. Gill uses this reframing technique to apply contemporary themes to such timeless stories and figures such as Medusa, Ares, and Persephone.
While reexamining these stories and characters, Gill explores mental health, relationship dynamics, trauma, gender, identity, and sexuality.

Purpose of the Project

The purpose of this exhibition is to invite students to contribute their own creative artworks to a campus wide collaborative exhibition. Inspired by the Gill's poetry, the exhibition would ask that works examine mental health using by reframing ancient mythological stories.

Through the use of art and myths, the exhibition creator hopes that the student works will encourage campus conversations about mental health.

UNC System & Mental Health

Students across the UNC system have reported struggling with mental health. One survey saw 47% of students report experiencing “severe symptoms of depression/anxiety."

To address community concerns on student mental wellness, the UNC System has stated that “the System is working to equip students with the skills required to understand and navigate their own mental health challenges, while also reducing the stigma around critical mental health conversations and empowering faculty, administration, staff, and peers to identify the early warning signs of a mental health crisis.”

This art exhibition is a student led contribution to the continual conversation of mental wellness in and around UNC's campus.

Mental Health & Art

Studies have shown that “participating in creative activities can help people cope with stress and despair and alleviate the burden of chronic mental illnesses.” Further, they have also shown that participants in art therapy demonstrated an improvement in “overall wellness or everyday anxiety” when interacting with the creative process and works.

By engaging in the creative process, student contributors are reaping some of the benefits of art therapy. Students and staff who interact with the exhibit will also recieve an improvement in overall wellness. These benefits add to those gained by furthering conversations focused on wellbeing.

Mental Health & Myths

The exhibition's use of mythology also has potential wellness benefits. Studies have demonstrated that fairy tales, legends, and myths can be used to elicit “positive benefits for emotional well-being.”

Additionaly, “as part of the healing process literature is often underrated” “Such stories enable people to “access …crucial issues and confront them in a symbolic form” notes Steven Walker, clinical social worker who specialized in childhood therapy in the UK.

Engaging with myths and recentering these old stories can allow students to explore areas of mental health that may be initially too uncomfortable to engage with headon.
